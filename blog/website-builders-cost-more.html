Why cheap website builders are costing rural businesses more than they think

Emile Holemans
Co-Founder  ·  March 2, 2026

Photo by Michaela Pereckas / Flickr (CC BY 2.0)

Wix. Squarespace. GoDaddy website builder. They all promise you a professional website in an afternoon. And technically, they deliver. You get a page on the internet with your business name on it. That part is real.

But there is a cost nobody puts on the pricing page. And for rural and small-town businesses specifically, that hidden cost is significant enough that I think these platforms are doing more damage than good.

I want to be direct about this because I see it constantly. A business owner spends a weekend building their own site on Squarespace, feels proud of it, and then wonders for the next two years why nobody is finding them online. The platform did not fail them. But it also never gave them what they actually needed.

"The question is not whether you have a website. The question is whether your website is doing anything for your business."

The template trap

Every website builder sells you templates. Clean ones, modern ones, ones that look great in a browser at 2am when you are excited about launching. The problem is that those templates were not built for a hardware store in Beaver Dam or a family restaurant in rural Belgium. They were built to be generic enough for anyone. And generic is the exact opposite of what builds trust in a small community.

When a local customer lands on your website and it looks like every other small business website they have ever seen, something subtle happens. They do not consciously think about it. But the impression lands as ordinary. And ordinary does not drive a decision to call, visit, or buy.

The SEO problem nobody warns you about

Website builders generate bloated code. That matters because Google measures how fast and how cleanly a page loads when deciding where to rank it. A custom-built site can score 95 out of 100 on Google's performance tools. A typical Wix or Squarespace site often scores in the 50s and 60s. That gap translates directly to where you appear in search results.

For a rural business where the entire addressable market might be one county, showing up on page two instead of page one of Google is not a minor inconvenience. It is customers who will never find you.

What you are actually paying

Squarespace costs around $23 per month on their basic business plan. Over three years that is about $830. Wix is similar. GoDaddy charges extra for almost every meaningful feature. By year three you have spent close to a thousand dollars on a website that ranks poorly, looks like everyone else, and you have spent dozens of hours maintaining it yourself instead of running your business.

A properly built custom website, optimized for your specific business and location, costs more upfront. But it works harder, ranks better, and does not require you to become a part-time web manager. For most rural businesses I have worked with, the return difference is not even close.

I am not against these tools in every situation. If you are testing an idea before you commit, a quick Wix site is fine. But if your business is real and your customers are real, your website should be too.
