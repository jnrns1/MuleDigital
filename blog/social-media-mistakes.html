Social Media

What every small town business gets wrong about social media

Emile Holemans
Co-Founder  ·  March 28, 2026

Photo by Kat Kellner / Flickr (CC BY 2.0)

Posting three times a week with no strategy is not a plan. It is digital busy work. And I have seen too many good businesses waste months on it before realizing nothing was moving.

Social media for small businesses is not about volume. It is not about following trends. It is not about being on every platform. It is about one thing: being consistently present where your actual customers are, with content that gives them a reason to trust you before they need you.

"The businesses that win on social media in small towns are not the ones posting the most. They are the ones that people feel like they actually know."

The platform problem

Most small businesses spread themselves too thin. They have a Facebook page that gets updated sporadically, an Instagram that went quiet six months ago, and maybe a TikTok account someone's nephew suggested. None of it has any traction because none of it has any focus.

Pick one platform and do it properly. For most rural businesses that is Facebook, because that is still where the majority of people over 35 in smaller communities spend time. For some it is Instagram, particularly if your work is visual. The point is to choose based on where your customers actually are, not where you think you should be.

What content actually works

People in small communities want to see the people behind the business. Not the products. Not the promotions. The people. A photo of your team on a Monday morning. A behind-the-scenes video of how you do what you do. A post acknowledging a local event or a long-time customer. That kind of content builds the kind of trust that a "20% off this weekend only" post never will.

Promotions have their place but they should be the exception, not the default. If every post is an offer, people start filtering you out mentally. If most posts feel like genuine community participation, the occasional promotion lands completely differently.

The consistency trap

You do not need to post every day. You need to post reliably. There is a real difference. Posting every day for two weeks and then disappearing for a month is worse than posting twice a week without fail. Algorithms reward consistency and so do people. They notice when you go quiet and they notice when you come back. The bar for small town social media is low enough that showing up regularly, even modestly, puts you ahead of most of your competition.
