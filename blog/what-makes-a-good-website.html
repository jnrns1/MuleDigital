What makes a good website for a local service business

Justin Reynolds
Founder  ·  March 3, 2026

Photo by bluesbby / Wikimedia Commons (CC BY 2.0)

I look at a lot of small business websites. It is basically a professional hazard at this point. And I want to say something that might sound harsh but is actually meant to be helpful: most of them are talking to the wrong person.

They are talking to themselves. They are full of "we have been serving the community since 1987" and "our team is passionate about quality" and "we take pride in our work." All of that might be completely true. None of it answers the question the person visiting the site is actually asking, which is: can you solve my problem, and should I trust you enough to call?

The five things a local service website actually needs

Start with what you do and where. The first thing someone should understand when they land on your site is what service you offer and what area you cover. Not buried in a paragraph. Right there at the top. "Plumbing and drain services in the greater Green Bay area" does more work in twelve words than a paragraph about your company history.

Your phone number needs to be visible without scrolling. On every page. At the top. On mobile especially. People looking for a local service are often ready to call. Do not make them hunt for the number. I have seen potential customers leave sites because they could not find contact information in ten seconds. They just left and called the next result.

Real photos matter more than stock photos. A photo of your actual truck, your actual team, or your actual completed work tells people more than any professionally shot generic image ever will. It proves you are real. It makes the business feel like a human operation rather than a website template. It also happens to look more authentic on mobile, which is where most of your traffic is coming from anyway.

"Your website does not need to be impressive. It needs to be convincing. Those are different things."

Social proof in some form. Reviews, testimonials, case studies, before and after photos — whatever makes sense for your type of business. People want evidence that other people trusted you and had a good experience. A single genuine testimonial from a named local customer does more work than a page of marketing copy.

A clear next step. Every page should end with an action. Call this number. Fill out this form. Get a free estimate. Do not leave people sitting on a page with no obvious direction. They will leave. Tell them what to do next and make it easy.

What most local business websites get completely wrong

They are designed for what the business owner wants to say, not for what the customer needs to hear. This is the single biggest issue and it shows up constantly. A business will spend paragraphs describing their certifications and their family history while never once addressing the specific problem their customer is dealing with right now.

Flip it. Start with the customer's situation. Acknowledge their problem. Then explain how you solve it. Then give them a reason to believe you can (reviews, credentials, photos). Then tell them how to contact you. That is the arc of a website that works.

The good news

You do not need a big, complicated website to do this well. A single well-written, well-structured page can outperform a ten-page site built without this thinking. The goal is not volume. The goal is clarity. Be clear about who you help, what problem you solve, and how to reach you. That is the whole job. Everything else is optional.
