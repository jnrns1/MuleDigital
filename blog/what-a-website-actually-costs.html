The truth about what a website actually costs a small business

Emile Holemans
Co-Founder  ·  March 14, 2026

Photo by hoh.coach / Flickr

There is a huge gap between what people think a website costs and what they actually end up paying. Both directions. Some pay too little and get nothing useful. Some pay too much and still get nothing useful. I want to put clear numbers on this because the confusion is costing businesses real money.

Let me break it down by the three most common routes a small business takes.

Route 1: DIY website builder

This is the Wix or Squarespace path. You pay roughly $16 to $30 per month, spend a weekend building something, and end up with a website that technically exists. Over three years you will spend $600 to $1,000 and somewhere between 40 and 80 hours of your own time managing it. The hidden cost nobody counts is those hours. If your time is worth $30 an hour conservatively, that is another $1,200 to $2,400 gone. And you still have a site that is hard to find on Google and looks like everyone else's.

Route 2: A freelancer from a job board

Anywhere from $200 to $800 for a one-off build. Sometimes you get something decent. More often you get a template with your logo dropped in, no SEO setup, and a developer who disappears after delivery. The low price feels like a win right up until you realize nobody is finding your site and you have no idea how to change anything on it.

Route 3: A professional agency

Most city agencies start their quotes at $5,000 and go up from there. For a rural business with a few thousand dollars to invest, that door is closed before the conversation starts. This is the gap that Mule Digital exists to fill.
